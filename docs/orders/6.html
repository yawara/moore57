Why a candidate

Order 6 = 2 · 3 is a candidate because it divides the following Theorem 7 maxima: 54.

From the paper (Macaj–Siran 2010)

Lemma 15 row pq = 6 (a₀ = 2, a₀(x_p) = 10, a₀(x_q) = 56) constrains order-6 elements; no starred row.

Theorem 2 (Makhnev–Paduchikh) reads the two order-6 groups via G = ⟨Y, t⟩ × X with |Y| ∈ {1, 3, 5, 7, 19, 21, 57} and (if X ≠ 1) Fix(X) ∈ {star, pentagon, Petersen, HS}:

  • S₃ = D₃: Y = ℤ₃ inverted by t, X = 1; |Y| = 3 ✓, condition (b) vacuous. Paper-allowed.
  • ℤ₆ = ℤ₂ × ℤ₃: Y = 1, X = ℤ₃; |Y| = 1 ✓. MP 2001 Lemma 3 cases (2) / (5) for p = 3 give Fix(ℤ₃) ∈ {singleton, Petersen}; condition (b) eliminates the singleton, but the Petersen branch survives. Paper-allowed.

The paper does not exclude either group of order 6.
